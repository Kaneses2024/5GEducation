The Power of 5G: What You Need to Know

Image by: Microsoft. (2024). Microsoft Copilot (Version 1.0). Microsoft. https://copilot.microsoft.com/

What is 5G?

Launched back in 2019, 5G is known as the fifth generation wireless technology and is also the latest advancement in cellular network technology. It's designed to provide significantly faster data speeds, lower latency, and greater capacity compared to its predecessor, 4G. With peak speeds reaching up to 10 - 20 gigabits per second (Gbps), 5G networks can handle more connected devices simultaneously, making it ideal for densely populated areas and the growing Internet of Things (IoT). This technology is expected to support a wide range of applications, from enhanced mobile broadband to mission-critical services like remote surgery and autonomous vehicles. Now, while there's no exact telling when 5G will be fully available globally, it's expected that it will be available to the majority of the population well beyond 2020 (Qulacomm, n.d.).

How Does it Work?

5G networks are built on several advanced technologies that work together to provide its next-gen features. According to Nordrum et al. (2017), one of the key technologies is the use of millimeter waves (mmWave), which are higher frequency bands between 30 and 300 gigahertz (GHz). These waves allow for faster data transmission but have shorter range and are susceptible to obstacles. Fortunately, this can be overcome by using small cells, which are low-power base stations that cover small geographical areas. These are typically deployed in dense urban environments to enhance coverage and capacity by offloading traffic from larger macro cells. Another crucial technology is Massive MIMO (Multiple Input Multiple Output), which involves using multiple antennas at both the transmitter and receiver ends to increase the capacity and efficiency of data transmission. However, if more antennas were to be installed, then there will be an increase in interference. This is where beamforming, another important technology, comes in. In short, it focuses a wireless signal towards a specific device rather than broadcasting it in all directions, improving signal strength and reducing interference (Nordrum et al., 2017).

Moreover, 5G requires high throughput and low latency which can be achieved through another technology called full duplex. This will allow for simultaneous transmission and reception of data in the same frequency, effectively doubling the capacity of networks (Nordrum et al., 2017). 5G also uses network slicing, which is a form of virtual network architecture that allows multiple virtual networks to be created on top of a common physical infrastructure (Flinders & Smalley, 2024), each slice being customized to meet the specific needs of different applications. Lastly, 5G relies on Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiplexing (OFDM), which modulates digital signals across various channels to minimize interference. In addition to using the OFDM principles, 5G incorporates the 5G New Radio (NR) air interface which enhances OFDM's flexibility and scalability. With this, it will allow 5G to provide more extensive coverage and support a broader range of use cases, reaching more people and connecting more devices effectively (Qualcomm, n.d.).

Why is it Important?

5G is positioned to revolutionize various industries and transform society by enabling new applications and services. Its high speeds and low latency are crucial for technologies like virtual reality (VR), augmented reality (AR), and autonomous vehicles, which require real-time data processing. 5G also has the potential to improve public safety and health services, such as smart cities and remote healthcare (GAO, 2020). Moreover, the increased capacity and efficiency of 5G networks can drive economic growth by supporting new business models and creating jobs. Overall, 5G is expected to be a cornerstone of the Fourth Industrial Revolution, bringing about significant advancements in connectivity and digital experiences (Verizon, 2019).

The Brief History of 5G

Early Development

The journey of 5G began in the early 2010s, with major telecommunications companies and research institutions laying the groundwork for this next-generation technology. Initial research focused on developing new radio frequencies, advanced modulation techniques, and improving spectral efficiency. By 2015, active preparations for 5G were underway, with the goal of achieving faster data speeds, lower latency, and higher device densities. A significant milestone in the development of 5G was the creation of the 5G New Radio (NR) by the 3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP). 5G NR is a radio access technology aimed at being the global standard for the 5G air interface. As mentioned before, it builds on orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing (OFDM), similar to what 4G LTE uses, but includes major enhancements to meet the varied needs of 5G. Additionally, the 3GPP specification 38 series details the technical aspects of 5G NR, positioning it as the successor to LTE.

The study of 5G NR within 3GPP started in 2015, with the first specification released by the end of 2017. During the ongoing standardization process, the industry began developing infrastructure in line with draft standards, leading to the first large-scale commercial launch of 5G NR at the end of 2018. Ultimately setting the stage for 5G's commercial deployment.

Initial Deployment

The first commercial 5G networks were launched in 2019. Verizon was the pioneer, rolling out 5G services in select areas of Chicago and Minneapolis, expanding its service a bit later to several other cities (Hollington, 2022). Other major carriers like Sprint, AT&T, and T-Mobile followed suit, expanding their 5G networks throughout the year. These early deployments primarily utilized high-frequency millimeter-wave (mmWave) technology, which offered impressive speeds but had limited coverage due to its short range.

Global Spread

By 2020, 5G technology began to spread globally, with many countries launching their own 5G networks. The technology continued to evolve, incorporating a mix of low-band, mid-band, and high-band frequencies to balance speed and coverage. As of now, 5G networks are being deployed worldwide, supporting a wide range of applications from enhanced mobile broadband to the Internet of Things (IoT) and mission-critical services.
